1 · The principle

Pixel art is the art of constraints

In most digital art a pixel is meaningless. In pixel art the opposite is true: every pixel is a decision you make on purpose. You work small, with a limited palette, and let the viewer's eye assemble the rest. That limitation isn't a weakness — it's the whole craft.

Why it worksOn a small canvas the viewer's brain snaps a few well-placed pixels into a clear shape. Give it just enough signal, and no noise.
2 · Sixty seconds of history

From 8-bit to 16-bit

Early consoles showed only a handful of colors, so 8-bit art looks flat and bold. The 16-bit era loosened the limits — more colors meant shading, highlights, and depth. Same mushroom, two eras:

5 · Guided practice

Your first reps — in Aseprite

Rep 1

Make a 16×16 canvas. Draw a simple heart or star in one color, then add one darker shade to the bottom-right edge.

✓ A clean shape, lit from one side.
Rep 2

Export it as a PNG scaled ×8 (File → Export) and drop it in your submissions folder.

✓ Upscaled PNG saved.
Graded assignment

One object, three shades

Render a small object (heart, gem, potion, coin) on a 16×16 canvas using exactly three shades of one color and a single light direction. Export ×8 to your submissions folder.

We grade for
  • Readable silhouette — recognizable as a shape.
  • Intentional pixels — no strays; deliberate edges.
  • One light source — shadow & highlight on consistent sides.
Recap

What to carry forward

  • Pixel art is the art of constraints — say more with fewer, clearer pixels.
  • Resolution & palette are creative choices you set before you draw.
  • Render in order: silhouette → base → shadow → highlight.
