1 · Pixel art is the art of constraints

In most digital art, a single pixel is meaningless — one of millions, invisible on its own. In pixel art the opposite is true: every pixel is a decision you make on purpose. You work on a tiny canvas, with a deliberately limited palette, and you let the viewer's eye assemble the rest. The skill isn't pushing more detail; it's choosing the fewest, clearest marks that read as what you intend.

This is why pixel art rewards restraint. A 16×16 character has roughly 256 cells to say everything — pose, costume, mood, light. Every pixel you add competes with the ones already there. Great pixel artists develop an instinct for what to leave out, the same way a good writer cuts words.

Hold this idea as your north star for the whole course: constraints are your design partner. When you feel stuck later, the answer is almost always to simplify, not to add.

Why it worksBecause the canvas is small, the viewer's brain does the heavy lifting — it "snaps" a handful of well-placed pixels into a recognizable shape. Your job is to give it just enough signal, and no noise.

2 · Sixty seconds of history

Early consoles could only display a handful of colors at once, on tiny sprites. That hardware limit is exactly why 8-bit art looks flat and bold — there was no room for subtle shading. The 16-bit era loosened the rules: more colors on screen meant artists could add shading, highlights, and depth, which is why a Super Nintendo sprite feels rounder and richer than an NES one.

Today you're not bound by old hardware — which means resolution and palette size are yours to choose. Common working sizes are 16×16 (icons, small characters), 32×32 (detailed characters), and 48–64px (bosses, key art). Smaller forces bolder, more readable choices; larger allows more nuance but demands more discipline.

Set the rule firstDecide your canvas size and palette before you draw, and stick to them. The constraint is what gives the piece its style.

3 · Worked example: build a heart, one decision at a time

Watch how a finished sprite is really a short sequence of intentional decisions. We'll build a small heart on a 16×16 canvas. Notice that shape comes first, color second, light last — that order matters.

That's the entire logic of rendering a simple object: a clean silhouette, a base, a shadow, a highlight — all driven by a single decision you make up front (where is the light?). You'll reuse this exact sequence on characters, props, and tiles for the rest of the course.

Common mistakesTwo traps to avoid early: (1) shading both sides of an object — pick one light source and commit; and (2) using pure black for shadows — it kills color. A dark, slightly desaturated version of your base reads far better.

5 · Guided practice

Two short reps in Aseprite to get your hands moving. Low pressure — the goal is comfort and the silhouette-first habit, not a masterpiece. Hit each checkpoint before moving on.

  1. Make a 16×16 canvas. Block a simple heart or star as a silhouette in one color. Squint at it — does the shape read instantly?
    ✓ Checkpoint: a clean, readable single-color shape.
  2. Pick a light direction and add one darker shade to the opposite edge, then one lighter shade where light hits. Three shades total.
    ✓ Checkpoint: the shape now looks slightly rounded, lit from one side.

Graded assignment

Apply the worked-example sequence on your own: render one small object (a heart, a gem, a potion, or a coin) on a 16×16 canvas, using exactly three shades of a single color and one consistent light direction. Export it as a PNG scaled ×8 and drop it in your submissions folder. Your Grading agent will review it against the rubric below — M0 is pass / revise, focused on the habit, not perfection.

CriterionWhat we look for
Readable silhouetteThe object is instantly recognizable as a shape, even before color.
Intentional pixelsClean placement — no stray or accidental pixels; edges look deliberate.
Single light sourceShadow and highlight sit on consistent, opposite sides.
Three-shade disciplineExactly base + shadow + highlight; no pure black; shades clearly distinct.
ExportUpscaled PNG, clearly named, in the submissions folder.

Recap

  • Pixel art is the art of constraints — say more with fewer, clearer pixels.
  • Resolution and palette are creative choices you set before you draw.
  • Render in order: silhouette → base → shadow → highlight, driven by one light source.
  • You're equipped with Aseprite; free fallbacks exist for every step.
