Value

If you master only one fundamental, make it this one. Value is what makes an image read — long before color gets any credit.
Module 3  ~25 min  Lesson 4
By the end of this lesson you will
  • Understand value — lightness and darkness, independent of color — and why it outranks every other fundamental.
  • Design with notan: strong, readable patterns built from just 2–3 big value shapes.
  • Use contrast to control where the viewer's eye goes (the focal point).
  • Work value-first: plan in grayscale, then lay color over the structure.
  • Complete a graded value-first illustration.

1 · Value is king

Value is the lightness or darkness of an area, regardless of its color. It is the single most important fundamental in all of visual art, because almost everything that makes an image read — form, depth, mood, focus — is carried by value, not hue. There's an old studio saying: "Value does the work, color gets the credit." A piece with strong values and dull color still reads; a piece with gorgeous color and weak values turns to mush.

Train your eye on the value scale below — a ramp from black to white. Learning to judge where a color sits on this scale (try squinting, which blurs away detail and color and leaves only value) is a skill you'll use every single time you pick a pixel.

Master studyTake three pieces of art or game screenshots you love and desaturate them to grayscale. You'll see the magic: the value structure alone carries the image, and most strong pieces resolve into just a few big value shapes.

2 · Notan — design in big value shapes

Notan is the Japanese idea of arranging light and dark masses. Before any detail or color, a strong image already works as a simple pattern of 2–3 values. The test is brutal and simple: if your big shapes don't separate clearly by value, the image won't read — no matter how much detail you add later.

Same composition, same shapes — the only difference is value separation. This is why pros thumbnail in three values first: get the notan right and the rest of the painting is just elaboration.

3 · Contrast directs the eye

Here's the lever notan hands you: the eye is drawn to the area of greatest value contrast. Where your lightest light meets your darkest dark, attention goes — automatically. That makes contrast your tool for building a focal point.

Why it worksSpend your strongest contrast on what matters most — a character's face, the key item. If everything is high-contrast, nothing stands out and the eye has nowhere to rest.

4 · Worked example: value before color

The professional order of operations: nail the values in grayscale, then add color on top. Color almost always rides on a value structure that was decided first.

Common mistakes(1) Values too close — the number-one beginner issue; everything sits in the mid-range and the image goes muddy. (2) Asking color to do value's job — a vivid hue at the wrong value still won't read. (3) Contrast everywhere — scatter your darks and lights evenly and you destroy the focal point.

5 · Guided practice

  1. Paint a 5-step value scale by hand in Aseprite (even jumps from near-black to near-white). Then do a tiny 3-value notan thumbnail of any scene.
    ✓ Checkpoint: even value steps; the notan reads as a scene in just 3 values.
  2. Take an existing sprite, desaturate it (or eyedrop its values), and check whether the big shapes still separate.
    ✓ Checkpoint: you can tell whether your sprite's values are doing the work — or hiding behind color.

Graded assignment

Make a small illustration or sprite scene designed value-first. Submit two images: (a) a 3-value grayscale notan plan, and (b) the final colored version that preserves that value structure, with a clear focal point created by contrast. A 32–48px canvas is ideal. Export both as PNGs scaled ×8 to your submissions folder.

CriterionWhat we look for
Value separationBig shapes read clearly apart in grayscale; no muddy mid-tone soup.
Notan planA genuine 3-value plan exists and the final follows it.
Focal pointThe strongest contrast lands on the most important spot.
Color rides valueColors sit at the right values; desaturating the final still reads.
ExportBoth images delivered, upscaled, clearly named, in the submissions folder.

Recap

  • Value outranks color — it carries form, depth, mood, and readability.
  • Design the notan first: 2–3 big value shapes that separate clearly.
  • Contrast directs the eye — spend your strongest contrast on the focal point.
  • Work value-first: grayscale plan, then color on top.
