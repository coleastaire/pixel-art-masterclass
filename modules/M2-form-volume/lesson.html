Form & Volume

A drawing is flat. A form looks like you could pick it up. This is how you turn shapes into volume.
Module 2  ~24 min  Lesson 3
By the end of this lesson you will
  • See every object as a combination of four basic solids: sphere, cube, cylinder, cone.
  • Understand that value describes how a surface turns toward or away from the light.
  • Tell the difference between a flat plane (hard value step) and a curved surface (smooth gradient).
  • Render a flat shape into a believable 3D form from a single light source.
  • Construct a more complex object from primitives, then complete a graded assignment.

1 · The four building blocks of form

Here's a secret that classical ateliers have taught for centuries: almost anything can be built from four simple solids — the sphere, the cube, the cylinder, and the cone. A head is a sphere and a box. A tree is a sphere on a cylinder. A character is a stack of cylinders and boxes. Learn to render these four convincingly and you can render anything, because you'll be drawing the underlying form, not just the outline.

Master studyPull up a still life or a character you admire and mentally break it into primitives. Where are the spheres, the boxes, the cylinders? Trained artists do this automatically — it's why they can draw the same object from any angle.

2 · How a surface turns

Form is created entirely by value — the lightness or darkness across a surface. The rule is simple: the more a surface faces the light, the lighter it is; the more it turns away, the darker. So the way a surface turns decides how the value behaves.

On the cube you can name three planes — the top (facing the light, lightest), the front (angled, mid), and the side (turned away, darkest). On the sphere those same values exist but blend into a ramp. Same principle, different surface.

3 · Worked example: from flat circle to sphere

Watch a flat disc become a form. The only decision driving everything is where the light comes from (here, upper-left).

Notice the parts of a rendered form, because you'll use these words constantly: the highlight (where light hits most directly), the core shadow (the darkest band where the surface turns fully away), the reflected light (a subtle lift on the shadow edge from bounced light), and the cast shadow (what the object throws onto the ground).

Common mistakesThree classics: (1) putting the highlight on the very edge — the rim of a sphere turns away from you, so the brightest spot sits inside the form; (2) making the reflected light as bright as the lit side — keep it subtle, or the form looks flat; (3) shading a curved surface with hard cube-like bands.

4 · Constructing from primitives

Real objects are just primitives combined. See the big forms first, render each as its own solid under the same light, then unify them. This snowman is nothing but two spheres and a cone — but because each piece is rendered as a form, the whole thing feels solid.

5 · Guided practice

Pick one light direction for both reps and commit to it — consistency is the whole game.

  1. Shade all four solids (sphere, cube, cylinder, cone) on small canvases from a single light source. Use 3–4 values.
    ✓ Checkpoint: each solid reads as 3D; the cube has hard plane steps, the round forms have smooth ramps.
  2. Pick a simple object (mushroom, tree, robot) and construct it from 2–3 primitives, rendering each as its form.
    ✓ Checkpoint: you can point to the underlying solids in your finished piece.

Graded assignment

Choose an everyday object (a mug, a barrel, an apple, a die). First, note which primitive solids it's made of. Then render it in pixels on a 24–32px canvas, showing clear 3D form from one light source using 3–5 values, including a cast shadow. Export a PNG scaled ×8 to your submissions folder. We're grading the illusion of volume — not detail or color.

CriterionWhat we look for
Reads as 3DThe object looks solid and pickup-able, not flat.
Consistent lightOne clear light direction governs every value on the object.
Surface logicCurved areas ramp smoothly; flat planes step cleanly. Highlight sits inside the form.
Value range3–5 distinct values plus a cast shadow; core shadow is the darkest on the form.
ExportUpscaled PNG, clearly named, in the submissions folder.

Recap

  • Everything is built from sphere, cube, cylinder, cone — render the form, not the outline.
  • Value describes the turn of a surface toward or away from light.
  • Flat planes = hard value steps; curved surfaces = smooth ramps.
  • Learn the parts: highlight, core shadow, reflected light, cast shadow.
